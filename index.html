Internal dashboards are great, aren't they?

Project brief: Create an internal dashboard for the AirSorted team to manage the cleaning and laundry for their properties and to complete the different tasks in order of priority

Target device: Desktop/Laptop

Duration: 2.5 weeks

My role: Creating the survey, carrying out interviews, contextual enquiry, sketching and ideation, running design studios, paper prototypes, digital wireframes, usability testing

The problem

AirSorted were using 6 different platforms to manage a single booking. This was time consuming and relied on verbal communication to make sure team members weren’t simultaneously working on the same booking.


Discovery and research

We began our research by carrying out a competitive analysis of other property management companies as well as management tools.


We then sent out a survey based on the management of a process from start to finish. We analysed the responses of the 122 respondents and carried out 6 interviews. We interviewed a variety of people including taxi companies, a printing company, and a picture framing company. These interviews helped us to establish how other businesses are managing similar processes to AirSorted. Having carried out this research, we carried out a contextual enquiry to look at AirSorted’s booking management process in detail. Two personas were created to represent the main users. We were then able to create user flows and a user journey which helped us to establish the pain points that needed to be addressed, as well as which features were the most relevant for the AirSorted team to have in their dashboard.


Design

In the ideation phase, we ran design studios with AirSorted and within our team.



Various different ways of displaying information were withdrawn and a comprehensive feature list was created. We created a MoSCoW board to help prioritise the potential features based on the user research and the business needs. Once we had our final feature list, we created paper prototypes and tested them.

Pin up



Testing revealed certain pain points which we were able to resolve and test again. We then created digital wireframes and put these into a clickable prototype for testing. We created a user journey for the new process in order to visualise the progression from the previous user journey and then visual design was the final stage. A style tile was created for the skinned mockups.

The Solution

An internal dashboard (named Airbase) was created. The dashboard enabled the properties and bookings to be managed from one platform. Airbase also included a team page displaying which bookings each team member was working on and a stats page displaying the business metrics over time.

Don't you think the Kindle app could use some socialising?

Project brief: Increase customer engagement within the Kindle App. People love reading and talking about books. They love to have access to books via the Kindle App. Bringing these two activities together within the app would create a more social experience for readers.

Target device: iPhone 6

Duration: 2 weeks

My role: Team Leader
Team management, project management, survey and interviews, affinity map, user flows, sketching and ideation, paper prototypes, digital wireframes, skinned mock ups, usability testing

View more View prototype

The problem

Kindle customers were unable to discuss what they were reading with others via the Kindle App.


Discovery and research

We started the research process by sending out a survey via social media networks, friends, and book club forums. Within a few days we received 143 responses, which were followed by 8 interviews with book lovers. The research informed us that people don’t want to schedule formal discussions about books. They prefer a natural and informal approach. They would like to interact with their friends discussing their favourite readings. Therefore one of our goals was to enable people to share and discuss books not only their eBooks. The survey results were put into an affinity map.

Affinity map

“reading is one of the general topics of my conversations, and I highly value friends’ recommendations, but I don’t like to organise events only talking about books” After the survey we started searching for various competitors offering similar services (Goodreads, Scribd, Medium, iBooks, Richard & Judy Bookclub). Surprisingly all other digital book platforms offer similar services. Therefore we started analysing sharing and chat apps (Spotify, Soundcloud, Whatsapp, Snapchat, Path, Tango). We realised that amongst the digital book platforms none offered a service of scanning, chatting and sharing books in real time. The next step of the research process was interviewing users, while using the kindle app (Contextual Inquiry). We interviewed Sarah who found the Kindle app particular difficult to use. She couldn’t navigate easily, as she struggled to find books she wanted or to add books to her collection. But one of her main points was that the app didn’t allow her to share books with their friends. Interestingly, after the interview she texted: “very frustrating experience!”.


Design

Three personas were created from our research to represent different users and to help focus our design work. The primary persona was Tom, a 30 year old personal trainer who reads to relax and escape. Tom isn’t in a book group but he regularly discusses books in face-to-face conversations. He enjoys browsing in his local bookstore but also checks out reviews and recommendations online. Tom represented the majority of our users in some way as all of our personas shared needs with Tom. The share and chat feature was central to our design and evolved significantly over the course of the project. To attract readers to the new social features, we needed to make the Kindle App the fastest and easiest way to share a book. We did extra research into mobile chat apps to understand how they tackled this design problem. We paid particular attention to apps that focused on easy sharing of music, photos and videos.

Pin up

We also needed to balance sharing within the Kindle App with sharing on readers' existing networks. It can be difficult to drive take-up of an app if it relies on all the people in a social group downloading it too. In early iterations we tried to give Kindle and external networks equal weight. This made the interface cluttered and confusing. We then moved to a much simpler design, which was appealing to users but we felt gave the Kindle network too little focus. In later prototypes, the Kindle network is clearly the priority, although other networks are also available.

Kindle profile sketches



Once we had a prototype in InVision, we carried out recorded usability tests with readers from our research pool, using Skype and Quicktime. The first test showed that the app was easy to learn. Our design was consistent, the tester recognised key features when they reappeared later in the app flow. We did realise we needed to pay more attention to context, only presenting the user with relevant options for their current flow. In some cases we could remove options entirely. For instance, if a user is scanning a book they might want to choose between 'save' and 'share'. But if they are scanning while composing a chat message, they probably want to add the book to their message, so we can take that action for them. Our tester felt scanning in general was too cumbersome. We simplified the feature in the next iteration and it worked much better for the next tester.


The solution

For this short project we focused on features that would appeal to the majority of the readers we contacted in our research: sharing books, scanning books, and chatting with their book-loving friends.

Final result



Wouldn't it be cool if Gousto was personal?

Project brief: Gousto recently partnered with a major supermarket group giving them access to a full range of ingredients. A website that will help people plan and manage their meals and ingredient purchases more efficiently is therefore needed.

Target device: Desktop/Laptop

Duration: 1 week

My role: Site map, navigation schema, competititve analysis, user flows, user journeys, designing check out process, digital wireframes

View more View prototype

The problem

Gousto interviewed its users regarding their e​ating habits. Users cited a lack of time due to work and family commitments as the main reasons for reducing the number of home cooked meals they make. Users also felt they planned poorly when purchasing food at stores and supermarkets ­ many choices were made on the fly. As a result they often stated that they threw out spoiled food that wasn’t eaten in time.


Discovery and research

A comparative analysis of competing businesses was carried out. Online competitors (Hello Fresh, Abel & Cole, Riverford and Just Eat) were analysed as well as two brick and mortar competing stores (Waitrose and Sainsbury’s local). The decision making, selection, location/layout, navigation and check out process were all compared. Particular attention was paid to negative parts of the experiences, in order to eliminate potential pain points for Gousto’s new site.


Design

Method and testing results: Design studios and user testing helped to establish pain points. A task analysis also focused on the user’s thought process. The open card sorting method was used to observe the potential categorisation of the individual products, including recipes, individual ingredients, tutorials, and menu packages.



Card Sorting



Site map and navigation schema: A site map was drawn up to provide the overall plan of the website. Content lists were included for more information about each category. A navigation schema then organised both the global and the common elements into a navigable structure. A user journey was created for the process of choosing meals for given days and purchasing the ingredients. User flows were created for identifying and removing ingredients, selecting meals for given days and purchasing the ingredients, and for the checkout process.


The solution

A new Gousto website was designed to enable users to order a variety of meals for given days, as well as any extra ingredients they require. The options to save particular recipes, or to upload their own recipes, were also designed.

Ever had paper recipes get messy and wish they were digital?

Project brief: Elsa is a 31 year old woman from Barcelona. She loves to cook but she currently prints the recipes her mother emails her from Spain and stores them in a folder. This process takes time and the papers disintegrate when she is cooking. She needs the recipes to be digitised whilst keeping their homely and handwritten feel.

Target device: Mobile App

Duration: 1 week (plus 1 week visual design)

My role: Interviews, paper prototyping, usability testing, visual design

View more View protoype

The problem

Elsa is hesitant to use a device as she doesn't want to have to touch a device when her hands are messy whilst cooking”


Discovery and research

Interviewing Elsa led to a mood board and a concept map about her being created.

Mood board



This concept map allowed me to link certain points to one another and to draw out key themes related to the problem raised. Finding out why the problem was a problem for Elsa was important, rather than just finding out what the problem was. As the interview progressed, I found out that the majority of Elsa’s recipes are Spanish recipes her mother emails to her. This is because she doesn’t really like a lot of English food. Printing the emails and storing them in a folder is time consuming for Elsa and the recipes are easily damaged. A user flow enabled me to dive deep in to Elsa’s thought process in order to create an app which would meet her needs and enable her to be pleased with her recipe storage.

User flow



Design

Paper prototyping, participatory design and user testing led to valuable feedback about certain confusing elements and navigational issues. Observing users hesitation points using the app on the paper prototype helped clarify which features needed work as well as listening to the user’s thoughts.



Paper prototypes



The Solution

The ‘RecAPPe my recipe’ app was designed to enable Elsa to paste the recipes her mother emails her (or occasionally recipes from the Internet) into an organised storage system on her mobile phone. The method page voiceover means Elsa would not have to touch the screen once she had started cooking - until she was ready to. The option to take a photo of the dish and send it via email was an additional feature as Elsa thought she would enjoy being able to send her mother photos of her finished dishes!

Final result
